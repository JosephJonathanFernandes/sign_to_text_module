## 2 LITERATURE SURVEY

### 2.1 LINGUISTIC SIGN-TO-TEXT TRANSLATION LIFECYCLE
The modern Sign Language Translation (SLT) paradigm functions as a sophisticated integration of high-fidelity computer vision, temporal sequence modeling, and generative linguistic processing. Engineering real-world viability necessitates a high-performance computational infrastructure—leveraging PyTorch’s dynamic execution cycles (Paszke et al., 2019) alongside hierarchical HDF5 data management to suppress prohibitive I/O bottlenecks (Boehm et al., 2019). Furthermore, architectural development must be fundamentally grounded in Deaf-centered design protocols to ensure absolute cultural and semantic accuracy (Bragg et al., 2019). The rapid evolution of these frameworks has been catalyzed by the deployment of massive, real-world benchmarks, such as the INCLUDE dataset (Sridhar et al., 2020), which provide the essential scale required to train robust, highly generalizable models.

### 2.1.1 COMPUTER VISION AND SPATIAL TOPOLOGY MAPPING
The foundational hurdle in SLT involves the extraction of high-fidelity articulatory features without relying on restrictive hardware sensors (e.g., sensory gloves). Production-grade pipelines increasingly adopt robust framework architectures like MediaPipe (Lugaresi et al., 2019) to standardize multi-modal inputs across heterogeneous environmental conditions (Halder & Tayade, 2021). To manage multi-signer morphological variability, modern systems implement regional reference-based normalization—such as neck-anchoring (Purbojo & Wijaya, 2025)—and calculate specific mathematical angles of distal phalanges to minimize the computational footprint (García-Gil et al., 2024). Prior to temporal modeling, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are frequently deployed to extract contextual environmental cues, effectively condensing raw frames into efficient spatial embeddings (Sridhar et al., 2020).

Because raw tracked coordinates lack inherent physiological context, state-of-the-art architectures utilize Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to map anatomical relationships. The foundational Spatial-Temporal Graph Convolutional Network (ST-GCN) models the human body as a non-Euclidean graph, explicitly acquiring motion patterns derived from skeletal joint connectivity (Yan et al., 2018). Advanced iterations, such as STAEGCN, introduce decoupled spatial-temporal attention to dynamically prioritize critical keypoints and bone topology, capturing both static grammatical postures and rapid articulatory transitions with exceptional precision (Xu et al., 2024).

### 2.1.2 SEQUENCE ALIGNMENT AND REAL-TIME STREAMING INFERENCE
While spatial mapping resolves isolated articulatory states, authentic sign language communication necessitates continuous, fluid motion tracking. Historically, Bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) networks served as the benchmark for resolving spatiotemporal transitions (Sridhar et al., 2020). However, the strict requirement for low-latency edge-device execution has driven a paradigm shift toward decoupling feature extraction before passing dense representations into parameter-efficient Bidirectional Gated Recurrent Units (BiGRUs) (Bhiri et al., 2024). Architectures utilizing MediaPipe-optimized GRUs structurally calibrate their internal update gates to selectively suppress redundant historical noise, thereby sustaining real-time inference on standard consumer hardware (Subramanian et al., 2022).

Determining the temporal boundaries of individual gestures within a continuous stream remains a critical bottleneck. Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC) mitigates this challenge by predicting sequence labels directly from unsegmented video streams (Graves et al., 2006). To prevent the misclassification of movement epenthesis (transitional, non-semantic gestures), signs are decomposed into linguistically derived sub-units to isolate core lexical articulators (Cooper et al., 2012). Recent frameworks address non-monotonic visual-to-text mapping by integrating CTC with multi-head attention mechanisms, leveraging hierarchical encoders for latent reordering—a methodology successfully validated on benchmark corpora like PHOENIX14T and CSL-Daily (Tan et al., 2024). For continuous video ingestion, SLT systems must execute "online" inference without suffering catastrophic context degradation (Zuo et al., 2024). This is typically achieved via a sliding-window protocol with recurrent state preservation; by extracting and passing the hidden state ($h_t$) across overlapping frame chunks, the system maintains a continuous temporal bridge, successfully balancing real-time execution velocity with bidirectional context.

### 2.1.3 NEURO-SYMBOLIC PIPELINES AND NLP INTEGRATION
To bridge the gap between abstract mathematical embeddings and spoken language, modern architectures employ hybrid neuro-symbolic frameworks. Initially, the neural network’s terminal classification layer condenses spatiotemporal features into task-specific logits, which are converted into probability distributions via a Softmax operator. Rather than hard-coding vocabulary directly into the neural weights, configuration-driven symbolic representations—such as lexicons and Knowledge Graph-based mappings—act as external rule bases to associate numerical outputs with linguistic tokens. This decoupled architecture enforces expert-defined logic to resolve minimal-pair ambiguities and mitigate semantic noise (Kezar et al., 2024; Cooper et al., 2012). Finally, deterministic decoding strategies, such as greedy search, are employed to synthesize these mapped symbolic representations into coherent textual outputs while rigorously preserving grammatical consistency.

At the syntactic level, the Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) functions as the architectural backbone required to overcome severe modality gaps, such as transitioning from Subject-Object-Verb (SOV) structural grammar to Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) phrasing (Zhang & Duh, 2024). This relies heavily on Scaled Dot-Product Attention, formulated as $\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$. To resolve complex reordering, multi-encoder topologies utilize cross-attention to facilitate hierarchical corrections (Pal et al., 2020). Given the severe scarcity of annotated sign language corpora, the field is actively transitioning from rule-based heuristics (Moryossef et al., 2021) to leveraging Large Language Models (LLMs) for pseudo-gloss generation, effectively eliminating manual annotation bottlenecks (Guo et al., 2024). Furthermore, injecting lightweight Adapters into frozen pre-trained networks enables parameter-efficient translation mapping while preserving foundational intelligence (Wong et al., 2024), and transfer learning facilitates broad classification across diverse regional dialects (Najib, 2025).

### 2.1.4 ROBUSTNESS, INFRASTRUCTURE, AND REAL-TIME DEPLOYMENT
To resolve "domain shift"—the precipitous performance degradation typically observed when models transition from controlled laboratories to unpredictable real-world environments—researchers integrate Domain-Adversarial Neural Networks (DANN). By introducing a secondary domain classifier, the feature extractor is optimized to simultaneously minimize translation error and maximize the classifier’s confusion, mathematically forcing the network to discard environmental noise and signer-specific variance (Ganin et al., 2016). Additionally, deterministic mathematical augmentations (e.g., geometric rotations and controlled noise injection) are applied to skeletal coordinates, ensuring invariant representation learning without the instability associated with GAN-based synthesis (Xin et al., 2024).

These architectural advancements rely entirely on massive, sentence-level benchmarks. Foundational datasets (Sridhar et al., 2020; Elakkiya & Natarajan, 2021) have been significantly expanded by large-scale translation corpora such as ISLTranslate (Joshi et al., 2023) and the comprehensive iSign benchmark (Joshi et al., 2024). To process this massive scale, pipelines adopt high-performance HDF5 containers, compressing landmark sequences into contiguous memory blocks to prevent GPU data starvation and I/O bottlenecks during training (Boehm et al., 2019).

To circumvent REST API latency in edge applications, real-time deployment additionally requires low-latency communication mechanisms to support continuous streaming inference. During deployment, Test-Time Adaptation minimizes entropy to safely adjust parameters during live execution, enabling robust, on-the-fly domain adaptation (Wang et al., 2021). Real-world profiling validates this architecture’s viability for real-time edge deployment: while MediaPipe feature extraction forms the primary computational bottleneck (~95.96 ms per frame), the subsequent mathematical preprocessing (~0.088 ms) and BiGRU sequence inference (~0.2878 ms per frame) introduce virtually zero computational overhead. These highly optimized metrics demonstrate the feasibility of continuous, fluid execution at over 10 FPS on standard consumer hardware, successfully bridging the gap between theoretical accuracy and practical real-time accessibility for the DHH community (Bhiri et al., 2024; Purbojo & Wijaya, 2025; Subramanian et al., 2022).
